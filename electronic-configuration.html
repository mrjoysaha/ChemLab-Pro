Introduction to Electron Configuration

The electronic configuration of an atom describes the distribution of its electrons across various energy levels (shells), subshells, and orbitals. Understanding this arrangement is key to predicting an element's chemical properties, reactivity, valency, and position in the periodic table.

Electrons reside in main energy levels called shells (designated by principal quantum number n = 1, 2, 3, 4... or letters K, L, M, N...). Each shell is divided into subshells (s, p, d, f), which in turn consist of one or more orbitals. An orbital is a region of space where there is a high probability of finding an electron, and each orbital can hold a maximum of two electrons.

Shell and Subshell Capacities:
  • Maximum electrons in a shell n = 2n2 (e.g., K-shell holds 2, L-shell holds 8, M-shell holds 18).
  • Subshell capacities: s holds 2, p holds 6, d holds 10, and f holds 14 electrons.

The Three Fundamental Rules

The filling of orbitals is governed by three primary principles:

1. Aufbau Principle

Aufbau is a German word meaning "building up". The principle states that electrons sequentially fill orbitals in order of increasing energy, starting from the lowest energy level. The order of filling is determined by the (n + l) rule, visualized by the diagonal rule pattern:

1s → 2s → 2p → 3s → 3p → 4s → 3d → 4p → 5s → 4d → 5p → 6s → 4f → 5d → 6p → 7s → 5f → 6d → 7p

Notice that the 4s orbital has lower energy than the 3d orbital, so 4s fills before 3d.

2. Pauli Exclusion Principle

Formulated by Wolfgang Pauli, this principle states that no two electrons in an atom can have the exact same set of four quantum numbers. Practically, this means that an orbital can hold at most two electrons, and they must have opposite spins (spin-up ↑ and spin-down ↓).

3. Hund's Rule of Maximum Multiplicity

When filling degenerate orbitals (orbitals belonging to the same subshell, like the three p or five d orbitals), electrons enter separate orbitals singly with parallel spins first, before pairing up. This minimizes electron-electron repulsion within the subshell.

Detailed Examples

Example 1: Carbon (Z = 6)

Carbon has 6 electrons. Following the rules:

  • First, fill 1s orbital with 2 electrons: 1s²
  • Next, fill 2s orbital with 2 electrons: 2s²
  • The remaining 2 electrons enter the 2p subshell. By Hund's rule, they occupy separate 2p orbitals with parallel spins: 2px¹ 2py¹

Full configuration: 1s² 2s² 2p²

Example 2: Iron (Z = 26)

Iron has 26 electrons. Following the Aufbau sequence:

Fill: 1s2, 2s2, 2p6 (10 e⁻), 3s2, 3p6 (18 e⁻). This matches the Argon core [Ar].

Next, the electrons fill 4s before 3d. We put 2 electrons in 4s (20 e⁻) and the remaining 6 electrons in the 3d orbitals.

Full configuration: 1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s² 3p⁶ 3d⁶ 4s² or [Ar] 3d⁶ 4s²

Anomalous Configurations: Chromium (Z=24) & Copper (Z=29)

Certain elements deviate from the Aufbau principle because half-filled (d5) and fully-filled (d10) subshells possess exceptional thermodynamic stability due to symmetrical distribution of charge and high exchange energy.

  • Chromium (Z=24): Expected: [Ar] 3d⁴ 4s². Actual: [Ar] 3d⁵ 4s¹. An electron shifts from 4s to 3d to create a stable half-filled d subshell.
  • Copper (Z=29): Expected: [Ar] 3d⁹ 4s². Actual: [Ar] 3d¹⁰ 4s¹. An electron shifts from 4s to 3d to form a highly stable fully-filled d subshell.
