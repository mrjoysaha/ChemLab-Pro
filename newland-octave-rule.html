Newland's Octave Rule

The law of octaves and early periodicity.

Historical Context

In 1865, English chemist John Newlands proposed a classification system for elements. By this time, elements were beginning to be identified in greater numbers, and Newlands arranged the 56 known elements in ascending order of their atomic weights.

The Law of Octaves

Newlands observed a repeating pattern: "When elements are arranged in increasing order of their atomic masses, the properties of every eighth element are a repetition of the properties of the first element."

Newlands drew a direct analogy to the octaves in music (where every eighth note on a scale is the same note, just an octave higher: Do-Re-Mi-Fa-Sol-La-Ti-Do or Sa-Re-Ga-Ma-Pa-Dha-Ni-Sa).

Newlands' Octave Grid

He arranged the elements in horizontal rows of seven, creating columns of similar elements:

Sa (1) Re (2) Ga (3) Ma (4) Pa (5) Dha (6) Ni (7)
H Li Be B C N O
F Na Mg Al Si P S
Cl K Ca Cr Ti Mn Fe

Detailed Examples

Example 1: The First Octave (Li and Na)

Starting from Lithium (Li) as Element 1:

1: Li → 2: Be → 3: B → 4: C → 5: N → 6: O → 7: F → 8: Na

Sodium (Na) is the 8th element. Both are alkali metals that are extremely soft, light, and reactive. Similarly, Potassium (K) is the 8th element after Sodium, sharing the same properties.

Critical Failures: Post-Calcium elements

After Calcium (Ca), the law broke down completely. For example, chromium (Cr) is placed under aluminum (Al), yet chromium is a transition metal with multiple oxidation states and does not react like the group 13 semimetal boron or metal aluminum.

Limitations of the Octave Rule

Newlands' peers ridiculed his musical analogy, and the Chemical Society refused to publish his paper initially. Key limitations include:

  • Applicable Only to Calcium: The periodicity worked reasonably well for the first 17 elements (up to Calcium), but failed completely for heavier elements.
  • Rigid assumptions: He assumed only 56 elements would ever exist and left no gaps for undiscovered elements.
  • Noble Gas Discovery: The discovery of Helium, Neon, and Argon added a new group (noble gases), meaning properties now repeated every ninth element, destroying the octave model.
