Mendeleev's Periodic Table

In 1869, Dmitri Mendeleev arranged the known elements by atomic weight into rows and columns so that elements with similar properties lined up in the same column. Where the pattern demanded an element nobody had discovered yet, he did something bold: he left a gap — and predicted, in detail, the properties the missing element would have.

The most famous prediction: "eka-silicon" → Germanium

One gap sat just below silicon. Mendeleev named the missing element eka-silicon ("one-beyond-silicon" in Sanskrit-derived notation) and predicted its properties from the trend of its neighbors. Fifteen years later, in 1886, Clemens Winkler discovered germanium — and it matched almost exactly:

Predicted (eka-silicon) vs. actual (germanium)
PropertyPredictedActual (Ge)
Atomic weight7272.6
Density5.5 g/cm³5.35 g/cm³
Formula of oxideEsO₂ (Es = eka-silicon)GeO₂
Boiling point of chloride< 100°C84°C (GeCl₄)

That level of accuracy — from a gap in a table, years before the element existed in anyone's lab — is the single strongest piece of evidence that the periodic law was describing something real about atomic structure, not just a convenient filing system.

Mendeleev's table still had a flaw: it was ordered by atomic weight, which occasionally puts elements out of step with their actual chemical family. That gets fixed by ordering on atomic number instead — see Modern Periodic Table.
