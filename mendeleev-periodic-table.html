Mendeleev's Periodic Law

In 1869, Russian chemist Dmitri Mendeleev published his periodic table, creating the first truly comprehensive and functional arrangement of the elements. Mendeleev formulated the Periodic Law, which stated:

"The chemical and physical properties of elements are periodic functions of their atomic weights."

By organizing the elements in order of increasing atomic weight and grouping elements with similar valencies (reactivity with hydrogen and oxygen) into columns, Mendeleev realized that similar properties returned at regular intervals.

Key Achievements

  • Gaps for Undiscovered Elements: Rather than forcing elements into columns where they did not fit, Mendeleev boldy left gaps in his table. He asserted that these gaps belonged to elements that had not yet been discovered, and named them using Sanskrit prefixes: Eka-boron, Eka-aluminum, and Eka-silicon.
  • Predictive Accuracy: He predicted the atomic weight, density, melting point, and oxide/chloride formulas of these unknown elements. When Gallium (1875), Scandium (1879), and Germanium (1886) were discovered, their properties matched Mendeleev's predictions almost perfectly.
  • Correcting Atomic Weights: Mendeleev used his periodic patterns to correct accepted atomic weights that were calculated incorrectly. For example, Beryllium's atomic weight was thought to be 13.5 (which would place it between Carbon and Nitrogen where there was no gap). Mendeleev argued it must have an atomic weight of 9.4, placing it correctly in Group 2.

Limitations of Mendeleev's Table

Despite its brilliance, Mendeleev's weight-based system contained logical contradictions:

  • Anomalous Pairs: To keep elements with similar properties in the same columns, Mendeleev had to place heavier elements before lighter ones. For example, Cobalt (\(58.9\)) was placed before Nickel (\(58.7\)), and Tellurium (\(127.6\)) before Iodine (\(126.9\)). Organised by mass, this was incorrect; organized by properties, it was correct.
  • Position of Hydrogen: Hydrogen exhibits properties of both alkali metals (Group 1) and Halogens (Group 17) and could not be given a single logical spot.
  • No Space for Isotopes: Because isotopes of an element have different atomic weights, a strictly weight-based table should place isotopes in separate slots, which made no sense chemically.

Detailed Examples

Example 1: Eka-Silicon vs. Germanium

Mendeleev predicted the properties of "Eka-silicon" (Es) in 1871. In 1886, Clemens Winkler discovered Germanium (Ge) and found:

  • Atomic Weight: Predicted \(72\) | Actual \(72.6\)
  • Density: Predicted \(5.5 \text{ g/cm}^3\) | Actual \(5.35 \text{ g/cm}^3\)
  • Formula of Oxide: Predicted \(\text{EsO}_2\) | Actual \(\text{GeO}_2\)
  • Boiling point of Chloride: Predicted \(< 100^\circ\text{C}\) | Actual \(84^\circ\text{C}\)
