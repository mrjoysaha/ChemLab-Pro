Döbereiner's Triad Law

In 1817, the German chemist Johann Wolfgang Döbereiner noticed that certain groups of three elements with similar chemical properties — which he called triads — showed a striking numerical pattern: the atomic mass of the middle element was close to the average of the other two. It was the first hint that atomic mass and chemical behavior were connected, decades before Mendeleev's periodic table.

Triad 1 — the alkali metals (Li, Na, K)

Soft, highly reactive metals that each form a +1 ion.

Atomic mass (amu)
ElementAtomic mass
Lithium (Li)6.94
Sodium (Na) — middle element22.99 (actual)
Potassium (K)39.10
Predicted Na = (6.94 + 39.10) / 2 = 23.02
Actual Na = 22.99  ·  difference 0.03 amu (≈0.13% error)

A remarkably accurate prediction from nothing more than the two neighboring masses.

Triad 2 — the halogens (Cl, Br, I)

Highly reactive nonmetals that each form salts with metals.

Atomic mass (amu)
ElementAtomic mass
Chlorine (Cl)35.45
Bromine (Br) — middle element79.90 (actual)
Iodine (I)126.90
Predicted Br = (35.45 + 126.90) / 2 = 81.18
Actual Br = 79.90  ·  difference 1.28 amu (≈1.6% error)

A slightly larger gap, but still a strong structural correlation — good enough to convince chemists that atomic mass wasn't arbitrary.

Döbereiner's law only worked for a handful of triads and broke down elsewhere — it was superseded by Newlands' Law of Octaves and then Mendeleev's periodic table, both covered elsewhere in this section.
