The Modern Periodic Law

In 1913, British physicist Henry Moseley discovered that the atomic number (\(Z\)), rather than atomic mass, is the fundamental property of an element. This resolved all the anomalous pairings in Mendeleev's table and led to the formulation of the Modern Periodic Law:

"The physical and chemical properties of the elements are periodic functions of their atomic numbers."

Structure of the Long Form

The modern periodic table is organized by electronic configuration, dividing elements into a grid of columns and rows:

  • Periods: 7 horizontal rows. The period number corresponds to the principal quantum number (\(n\)) of the outermost shell being filled.
  • Groups: 18 vertical columns. Elements in the same group have the same number of valence electrons, giving them highly similar chemical reactivities.
  • Blocks: The table is divided into four subshell blocks:
    • s-block (Groups 1-2 + He): Filling of \(s\) subshell.
    • p-block (Groups 13-18 minus He): Filling of \(p\) subshell.
    • d-block (Groups 3-12): Transition metals, filling of \(d\) subshell.
    • f-block (Lanthanides & Actinides): Inner transition elements, filling of \(f\) subshell.

Fundamental Periodic Trends

Organizing elements by atomic number reveals regular, repeating patterns in physical and chemical properties across periods and down groups:

1. Atomic Radius

The distance from the center of the nucleus to the outermost stable electron shell.

  • Across a Period: Decreases. The atomic number increases, adding more protons. This increases the effective nuclear charge, pulling the electron shells closer to the nucleus.
  • Down a Group: Increases. Each step down adds a completely new electron shell, shielding outer electrons and expanding the atomic radius.

2. Ionization Energy (IE)

The minimum energy required to remove the most loosely bound electron from a neutral gaseous atom.

  • Across a Period: Increases. The nuclear pull is stronger and atomic size is smaller, making electrons harder to remove.
  • Down a Group: Decreases. Outer electrons are further away in higher shells, making them easier to strip.

3. Electronegativity

A measure of the tendency of an atom to attract a bonding pair of electrons.

  • Across a Period: Increases. Nonmetals have a high nuclear charge and small size, strongly attracting bonding electrons.
  • Down a Group: Decreases. Large atomic size shields the positive nucleus, reducing its ability to attract outer bonding pairs.

Detailed Examples

Example 1: Alkali Metals Reactivity Trend

In Group 1 (Alkali Metals), chemical reactivity increases down the group:

\text{Li} < \text{Na} < \text{K} < \text{Rb} < \text{Cs}

Because their single valence electron is further from the nucleus in heavier elements (lower ionization energy), it is lost much more easily during chemical reactions. Cesium reacts instantly and explosively with water, whereas lithium reacts calmly.

Example 2: Halogens Reactivity Trend

In Group 17 (Halogens), chemical reactivity decreases down the group:

\text{F} > \text{Cl} > \text{Br} > \text{I}

Halogens react by attracting an extra electron to complete their octet. Fluorine, being the smallest atom in the group with the highest electronegativity, attracts electrons with extreme strength, making it the most reactive nonmetal on the periodic table.
