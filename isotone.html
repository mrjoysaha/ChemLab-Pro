What isotones are and why they share neutron counts.

What is an Isotone?

Isotones are nuclides (different types of atoms) that have the same number of neutrons (N) in their nuclei, but a different number of protons (different atomic number Z) and a different mass number (A).

Because they have different atomic numbers, isotones represent completely different chemical elements. Consequently, they share absolutely no similarities in chemical properties, unlike isotopes. They are grouped together solely based on their neutron counts, which is useful in nuclear physics for studying nuclear stability shell models.

Neutron Count Formula:

Neutrons (N) = Mass Number (A) - Protons (Z)

Detailed Examples

Example 1: The N = 8 Isotone Group

These three distinct nuclides are isotones because they all contain exactly 8 neutrons:

  • Carbon-14 (146C): Protons (Z) = 6, Mass (A) = 14. Neutrons (N) = 14 - 6 = 8.
  • Nitrogen-15 (157N): Protons (Z) = 7, Mass (A) = 15. Neutrons (N) = 15 - 7 = 8.
  • Oxygen-16 (168O): Protons (Z) = 8, Mass (A) = 16. Neutrons (N) = 16 - 8 = 8.

Carbon is a solid metal-like nonmetal, Nitrogen is a diatomic gas making up 78% of our atmosphere, and Oxygen is a highly reactive gas supporting life. Their chemical behaviors are totally distinct, yet their nuclei contain the same number of neutrons.

Example 2: The N = 16 Isotone Group

Another classic series showing elements from Periods 3:

  • Silicon-30 (3014Si): Neutrons (N) = 30 - 14 = 16.
  • Phosphorus-31 (3115P): Neutrons (N) = 31 - 15 = 16.
  • Sulfur-32 (3216S): Neutrons (N) = 32 - 16 = 16.
