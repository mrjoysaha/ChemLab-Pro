What isomers are and how nuclear and chemical isomers differ.

The Dual Meanings of "Isomer"

In science, the word isomer (from Greek isos "equal" + meros "part") is used in two very distinct contexts depending on whether you are studying the structure of the atomic nucleus or the structure of molecules:

  1. Nuclear Isomers: States of the same atomic nucleus (same atomic number \(Z\) and same mass number \(A\)) that exist in different energy states. One state is the stable ground state, and the other is a long-lived excited state called a "meta-stable" state.
  2. Chemical Isomers: Molecules that have the exact same molecular formula (same number of each atom type) but have different structural arrangements, bonding sequences, or shapes.

Nuclear Isomers

A nuclear isomer is a meta-stable state of an atomic nucleus caused by the excitation of one or more nucleons (protons or neutrons) to a higher energy level. These meta-stable isomers are designated with a letter "m" next to their mass number (e.g., \(^{99m}\text{Tc}\)).

Unlike standard excited states that decay in fractions of a nanosecond, meta-stable nuclear isomers can persist for hours or even days. They eventually return to the ground state through a process called isomeric transition, emitting energy in the form of a gamma-ray photon (\(\gamma\)) without changing their atomic or mass numbers.

Chemical Isomers

In chemistry, isomers are split into two major categories:

  • Structural (Constitutional) Isomers: Atoms are bonded in completely different orders. For example, the chain arrangement of carbons can be straight or branched.
  • Stereoisomers: Atoms have the same bonding sequence but differ in how they are oriented in 3D space (e.g., cis vs trans arrangements).

Detailed Examples

Example 1: Technetium-99m (Nuclear)

Technetium-99m (\(^{99m}_{43}\text{Tc}\)) is a meta-stable nuclear isomer of Technetium-99. It is the most widely used radioactive isotope in medical diagnostics worldwide.

  • It has a half-life of 6 hours, which is perfect for medical scans (gives enough time for imaging, but decays rapidly enough to minimize radiation exposure).
  • It decays to the ground state \(^{99}_{43}\text{Tc}\) by emitting a single low-energy gamma photon (\(140 \text{ keV}\)), which is easily detected by gamma cameras.

^{99m}_{43}\text{Tc} \xrightarrow{\text{Isomeric Transition}} ^{99}_{43}\text{Tc} + \gamma

Example 2: Butane vs. Isobutane (Chemical)

Both butane and isobutane share the chemical formula \text{C}_4\text{H}_{10}, but their atoms are bonded differently:

  • n-Butane: A straight chain of 4 carbon atoms. Boiling point: \(-0.5^\circ\text{C}\).
  • Isobutane (2-methylpropane): A branched chain with a central carbon bonded to three methyl groups. Boiling point: \(-11.7^\circ\text{C}\).

Because of this difference in structure, isobutane is more volatile and is often used as a refrigerant or propellant.
