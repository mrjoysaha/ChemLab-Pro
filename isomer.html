Atomic Structure

Isomer

A nuclear isomer is a nucleus with the same atomic number and the same mass number as another form of that nuclide, but sitting in a different, longer-lived nuclear energy state. Same protons, same neutrons, same element — just a nucleus temporarily "stuck" in an excited configuration before it settles down.

The most widely used example in medicine is Technetium-99m, where the "m" stands for metastable. It decays to ordinary Technetium-99 by releasing its excess energy as a gamma ray, without changing its atomic or mass number — a process called isomeric transition:

99m43 Tc Isomeric Transition → 9943 Tc + γ

Because it emits gamma rays without emitting particles (no alpha or beta decay), Tc-99m passes cleanly out of the body while still being easy to detect from outside with a gamma camera — which is exactly why it's the workhorse isotope behind most nuclear medicine scans.

Isomers vs. isotopes vs. isobars vs. isotones are easy to mix up — see the other Atomic Structure pages in the menu for how each one differs.
