What is an Isobar?

Isobars are atoms of different chemical elements that have the same mass number (A) but different atomic numbers (Z). In other words, isobars contain the same total number of nucleons (protons + neutrons), but the distribution between protons and neutrons is different.

Because they have different numbers of protons, isobars always represent different elements and exhibit completely different chemical properties. Despite having different subatomic mixtures, their total nuclear mass is almost identical (differing only by negligible mass differences between protons/neutrons and binding energies).

Detailed Examples

Example 1: The A = 40 Isobar Group

The elements Argon, Potassium, and Calcium form a well-known isobaric triplet with a mass number of 40:

  • Argon-40 (4018Ar): 18 protons, 22 neutrons. Total nucleons = 40. (Noble gas)
  • Potassium-40 (4019K): 19 protons, 21 neutrons. Total nucleons = 40. (Highly reactive alkali metal)
  • Calcium-40 (4020Ca): 20 protons, 20 neutrons. Total nucleons = 40. (Alkaline earth metal)

These elements are located adjacent to each other on the periodic table, yet are physically and chemically distinct. Calcium-40 makes up bones, Potassium-40 is radioactive and found in bananas, and Argon-40 is an inert gas.

Example 2: The A = 14 Isobar Pair

This pair represents a classic beta decay relationship:

  • Carbon-14 (146C): 6 protons, 8 neutrons.
  • Nitrogen-14 (147N): 7 protons, 7 neutrons.

When Carbon-14 undergoes beta decay, one of its neutrons turns into a proton, emitting an electron. The atomic number increases from 6 to 7 (Carbon becomes Nitrogen), but the mass number remains exactly 14.
