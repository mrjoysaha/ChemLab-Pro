Atomic Number

What the atomic number is and why it defines an element.

Definition of Atomic Number

The atomic number (represented by the symbol Z) of a chemical element is the number of protons found in the nucleus of every atom of that element. Because protons carry a positive charge (+1e), the atomic number also represents the net positive electric charge of the nucleus.

In an ordinary, electrically neutral atom, the number of negatively charged electrons surrounding the nucleus is exactly equal to the atomic number. Thus, Z also determines the electron configuration, which directly dictates how the element reacts and binds with other substances.

Key Formula:

Mass Number (A) = Atomic Number (Z) + Neutron Number (N)

Why Z Defines the Element

An element's identity is defined exclusively by its atomic number. If you change the number of neutrons in a nucleus, you get an isotope of the same element. If you change the number of electrons, you get an ion of the same element. But if you change the number of protons, you create an entirely different element.

For example, any atom with exactly 6 protons is carbon, regardless of how many neutrons or electrons it has. If it gains a 7th proton, it instantly becomes nitrogen.

Moseley's Law and History

Prior to 1913, elements were ordered in the periodic table by their atomic weight. This led to several anomalies, such as placing Iodine after Tellurium despite Tellurium having a higher atomic weight, to keep elements with similar properties grouped together.

In 1913, British physicist Henry Moseley studied the frequencies of characteristic X-rays emitted by different elements when bombarded with high-energy electrons. He discovered a mathematical relationship (Moseley's Law):

√ν = a(Z - b)

Where ν is the frequency of the X-ray line, and a and b are constants. Moseley proved that the atomic number corresponds directly to the physical positive charge of the atomic nucleus, providing a solid scientific justification for organizing elements by atomic number rather than mass.

Detailed Examples

Example 1: Carbon-12 vs Carbon-14

Carbon has an atomic number Z = 6, meaning every carbon atom has 6 protons and 6 electrons (if neutral).

  • Carbon-12 has a mass number A = 12. The number of neutrons is N = A - Z = 12 - 6 = 6 neutrons.
  • Carbon-14 is radioactive and has a mass number A = 14. The number of neutrons is N = 14 - 6 = 8 neutrons.

Both behave identically in chemical reactions, but Carbon-14 is physically heavier and unstable.

Example 2: Sodium Cation (Na⁺)

Sodium has atomic number Z = 11. A neutral sodium atom has 11 protons and 11 electrons. When sodium reacts, it loses one valence electron to form a stable octet, resulting in a sodium ion (Na+):

  • Protons: Z = 11 (positive charges)
  • Electrons: 11 - 1 = 10 (negative charges)
  • Net Charge: 11 - 10 = +1

Its atomic number remains 11; it is still sodium, but carries a positive charge.
